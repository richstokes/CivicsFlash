## Amendments Referenced

General reminder: the U.S. Constitution has twenty-seven (27) amendments. Changes are made through amendments or the amendment process, and the Bill of Rights protects the basic rights of Americans and people living in the United States.

| Amendment | Reminder |
| --- | --- |
| 1st Amendment | Protects freedoms listed in the questions: freedom of expression, freedom of speech, freedom of assembly, freedom to petition the government, and freedom of religion. |
| 2nd Amendment | Protects the right to bear arms. |
| 10th Amendment | Powers not given to the federal government belong to the states or to the people. |
| 14th Amendment | Persons born or naturalized in the United States, and subject to its jurisdiction, are U.S. citizens. People can become U.S. citizens by being born in the United States under the conditions set by the 14th Amendment, by naturalizing, or by deriving citizenship under conditions set by Congress. |
| 15th Amendment | A male citizen of any race can vote. All men got the right to vote after the Civil War, during Reconstruction, with the 15th Amendment, in 1870. |
| 16th Amendment | Paying federal taxes is required by law and by the U.S. Constitution; taxes fund the federal government and are a civic duty. |
| 19th Amendment | Any citizen can vote, women and men. All women got the right to vote in 1920, after World War One, with the 19th Amendment. |
| 22nd Amendment | The president can serve only two terms, to keep the president from becoming too powerful. |
| 24th Amendment | You do not have to pay a poll tax to vote. |
| 26th Amendment | Citizens eighteen (18) and older can vote. |

## Key Dates in America's History

| Date or Period | Reminder |
| --- | --- |
| 1775-1783 | American Revolution, the Revolutionary War, or the War for American Independence. Americans fought to win independence from Britain. |
| July 4, 1776 | The Declaration of Independence was adopted. It says America is free from British control, says all people are created equal, and identifies inherent rights and individual freedoms. |
| 1787 | The U.S. Constitution was written. It forms the government, defines powers and parts of government, and protects the rights of the people. |
| 1803 | The United States bought the Louisiana Territory from France. This doubled the size of the United States. |
| 1812-1815 | War of 1812. James Madison was president during the War of 1812. |
| 1861-1865 | The Civil War, the U.S. war between the North and the South. It ended slavery; important events include Fort Sumter, the Emancipation Proclamation, Gettysburg, Vicksburg, Appomattox, and Lincoln's assassination. |
| 1870 | All men got the right to vote after the Civil War, during Reconstruction, with the 15th Amendment. |
| 1920 | All women got the right to vote after World War One, with the 19th Amendment. |
| 1929 | The Great Depression started with the Great Crash or stock market crash of 1929. It was the longest economic recession in modern history. |
| 1941 | The United States entered World War II after the bombing of Pearl Harbor, when Japan attacked Pearl Harbor. |
| 1950-1953 | Korean War. The United States entered to stop the spread of communism; Dwight Eisenhower was president at the end of, or during, the Korean War. |
| September 11, 2001 | Terrorists attacked the United States, including the World Trade Center in New York City, the Pentagon in Arlington, Virginia, and a plane that crashed in a field in Pennsylvania. |

## Congress and Government

| Part | Key Info |
| --- | --- |
| Three branches | Legislative, executive, and judicial; or Congress, president, and the courts. Separation of powers, checks and balances, and three branches keep one part from becoming too powerful. |
| Congress / Legislative branch | Writes laws, declares war, and makes the federal budget. Congress has two parts: the Senate and the House of Representatives. |
| Senate | One hundred (100) U.S. senators. Each state has two senators for equal representation; senators serve six (6) years, represent the citizens or people of their state, and are elected by citizens from their state. |
| House of Representatives | Four hundred thirty-five (435) voting members. Members serve two (2) years, represent citizens or people in their congressional district, and are elected by citizens from their district; states have more representatives because of their population, and shorter terms help representatives more closely follow public opinion. |
| President / Executive branch | The president is elected for four (4) years, signs bills into law, vetoes bills, enforces laws, is Commander in Chief, is chief diplomat, and appoints federal judges. The Cabinet advises the president, and the vice president becomes president if the president can no longer serve. |
| Electoral College | Decides who is elected president and provides a compromise between the popular election of the president and congressional selection. |
| Judicial branch | Includes the Supreme Court and federal courts. It reviews laws, explains laws, resolves disputes about the law, and decides if a law goes against the U.S. Constitution. |
| Supreme Court | Highest court in the United States, with nine (9) seats. Five (5) justices are usually needed to decide a case; justices serve for life, until retirement, to be independent of politics and limit outside influence. |
| Federal and state powers | Federal-only powers include printing paper money, minting coins, declaring war, creating an army, making treaties, and setting foreign policy. State-only powers include providing schooling and education, police protection, fire department safety, driver's licenses, and zoning or land use approval. |
